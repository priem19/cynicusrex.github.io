Fractional reserve banking.

Even if there's a cap of 21 million bitcoin, and if we assume the code won't be altered, it does not stop fractional reserve banking. Just as governments went off the gold standard, they can get off the bitcoin standard. And just as banks lend out money they don't have, they can lend out bitcoin they don't have. Which already happens, a lot. Since most people can't be bothered to get their money of an exchange into their own wallet, they don't actually own their digital coins. As a result, exchanges can do whatever financial shenanigans because they know their customers won't do so.

This debate of sound money vs. fiat money isn't new. In fact, one of the most reverberating speeches in US history was centered on exactly the same problem: William Jennings Bryan's Cross of Gold speech ().

Since bitcoin hasn't delivered on its initial promises, it caused a periodical change of narrative. Currently the stock-to-flow theory or the deflationary aspect is its final vestige. ‚ÄúInflation is theft‚Äù is something that instinctively feels right. Let's say you had 10000 in savings, while earning 1000 a month. If due to inflation your savings buy only 9000 worth of stuff, you basically worked a month for free. That rubs me in the wrong way as well. However, things aren't so cut-and-dry, no matter how much you want it to be (or I wanted it to be). While saving is a virtue, hoarding isn't. And there's a thin line between the two; prudence can turn into greed remarkably quick. Moreover, on a deflationary currency there would hardly be any significant technological progress, lifting millions from poverty. It doesn't matter if the currency has a limited supply or not because it doesn't stop fractional reserve banking. Which is what instigated the creation of bitcoin in the first place.
